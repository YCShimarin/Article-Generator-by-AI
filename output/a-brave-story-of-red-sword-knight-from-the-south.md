# A brave story of red sword knight from the south

## Ember in the Ashes

The air in the southern lowlands tasted of cold ash and distant rain. Kaelen moved through the skeletal remains of Oakhaven, his boots crunching on blackened timbers and frosted leaves. The red sword at his hip felt impossibly heavy, its usual warm hum silent, as if mourning with the land.

He found her by the charred hearth of what was once a cottage—a girl, no older than seven, clutching a ragged doll. She watched him with eyes too old for her face, reflecting the grey sky.

“They took the men,” she said, her voice a dry leaf scraped over stone. “My father went to the road. He didn’t come back.”

Kaelen knelt, the armor cold against his thighs. He didn’t offer empty words. Instead, he unsheathed the sword, not in threat, but to rest the flat of the blade across his palms. The steel, etched with ancient southern runes, began to glow—a soft, steady ember-light in the gloom. It was a small warmth, but it was a beginning.

The girl’s breath caught. She reached out, not for the doll, but toward the light.

“It remembers fire,” Kaelen said quietly. “So do we.”

He left her with a strip of dried meat and a promise he prayed he could keep. As he stood, the ember in the steel pulsed once, fiercely. The northern shadows were gathering beyond the burnt fields, but here, in this broken place, a single, stubborn warmth had been rekindled.

He touched the hilt, feeling the faint, familiar pulse against his palm. The path ahead was long and dark, but he was a knight of the south. He carried the ember. And embers, given breath, could start anew.

## Whispers of the Blighted Moor

The Blighted Moor did not welcome travellers. It swallowed them in a silence so thick it pressed against the ears, a silence broken only by the wet *squelch* of mud drawing at Sir Kaelen’s boots and the weary pant of his mare, Lyra. The air was a stew of peat-rot and something sweeter, more organic—the smell of decay that had never known a clean wind. Kaelen, whose red sword—a legacy from the sun-baked hills of his southern home—hung heavy at his hip, felt the chill not in his bones but in his spirit. The vibrant gold of his homeland’s wheat fields was a memory fading against this expanse of bruised grey and stagnant pools that mirrored a perpetually overcast sky.

Lyra shied, her ears pricked. Kaelen laid a steadying hand on her neck, his calloused fingers finding the familiar warmth of her flesh beneath the grime. “Easy, girl,” he murmured, his voice low to avoid scattering the heavy quiet. “Just the wind.”

But it wasn’t the wind. The whispers began as a susurration, a million dry reeds scratching together, and then they coalesced into words—not in any tongue he knew, but in the language of loss and longing. They curled around the standing stones, half-sunken and lichen-eaten. *Lost… cold… stay…*

He clenched his jaw, the action tightening the scar that pulled at his cheek. His knuckles whitened on Lyra’s reins. The whispers spoke of the moor’s sorrow, of souls unmade by its slow, blighting touch. For a fleeting second, the weight of his quest—the distant, desperate call for a knight of the south—seemed a fragile thing against this ancient, hungry stillness.

He drew the red sword. Not for an enemy he could see, but for the feel of it. The familiar balance, the pommel warmed by his grip, the faint, comforting hum of well-tempered steel. It was a piece of home, a slash of defiant colour in this monochrome world.

“I am not staying,” he said to the moor, his voice clear and strong, a stone dropped in the still pond of whispers. The sound seemed to startle the mist. “My road is north.”

He sheathed the blade, urged Lyra forward into a deeper patch of sucking mud. The whispers did not cease, but they changed their tune, no longer a plea and now a dirge for his inevitable failure. Kaelen let them flow over him, his eyes fixed on a distant, dark ridge. The bravery he carried was not a shout, but this: the deliberate, muddy step forward, into the heart of the whispering dark.

## The Oath of Crimson Steel

The dust of the Southern Marches clung to Kaelen’s brow, tasting of iron and sun-baked stone. Before him, driven into the earth like a prayer, was the sword. Its steel was the colour of a deep, healing wound, the crossguard wrapped in worn leather, the pommel a smooth, blood-warmed stone. This was the Crimson Steel, and it had waited for him.

He did not reach for it at once. Instead, he knelt, the heat of the ground seeping through his trousers. He thought of the villages nestled in the canyon folds, of children with eyes the colour of the desert sky at dusk. He thought of the quiet, the peace that was not a given, but a thing guarded by men and women with tired hands and simple hearts.

“It is heavier than I remembered,” he murmured, his voice raw. The words were for the sword, but they were also for the old smith who had placed it here, for the mother who had kissed his scarred cheek and said nothing of fear.

His hand, calloused and sure, finally closed around the worn leather grip. The metal was not cold, but alive with the day’s heat, thrumming against his palm. As he drew it free, a low sound echoed—not a ring, but a sigh, as if the earth itself were releasing a held breath.

“The south does not yield,” he whispered, the oath forming not in grand pronouncement but in a solid, private truth. He pressed the flat of the blade against his forearm, feeling its weight, its balance, its promise. A single, perfect drop of sweat traced a path down his spine.

He stood, the sword now an extension of his will, its crimson length catching the brutal light. The wind rose, carrying the scent of distant rain and closer dust. Somewhere beyond the mesas, shadows were gathering. Kaelen turned his face toward them, the oath settled in his bones, a warm, iron certainty. The path was clear.

## Shadows Over Sunstone Hold

The sun hung low over Sunstone Hold, gilding the ancient granite walls until they seemed to hold the day’s last warmth within them. In the stable yard, the air smelled of sun-baked hay, pine tar, and the faint, sweet musk of horses. Sir Kaelen, his red sword—a blade forged in the southern fires of his homeland—resting at his hip, ran a hand along the neck of his warhorse, Torch. The animal’s ears flicked, a low tremor running through his flank.

“Easy, lad,” Kaelen murmured, his voice a low rumble that carried the soft cadence of the southern marches. He squinted against the light, his gaze sweeping the high curtain walls and the deep, blue-tinged shadows pooling in the western archways. The Hold was quiet, too quiet for late afternoon. No children’s shouts from the bailey, no clatter from the smithy. Just the buzz of late-flying bees and the distant cry of a hawk.

The stable boy, Joren, appeared from the gloom of a stall, clutching a brush. “They’re uneasy,” the boy said, his eyes wide. “Even old Branwen, and she’s seen more winters than the keep’s cook.”

Kaelen nodded, the movement tight. He felt it too—a stillness that wasn’t peace, a chill that had nothing to do with the coming dusk. It was the same feeling he’d carried through the Blackroot Pass, the sense of being watched from the high places. He laid his palm flat against Torch’s shoulder, feeling the powerful muscle coiled beneath the skin.

“Get the lanterns lit early tonight,” Kaelen said, his tone casual, but his eyes never left the shadow beneath the west gatehouse. “And tell the sentries to watch the high trail, not just the road.”

Joren hesitated, then nodded, the brush trembling in his hand. As the boy hurried off, Kaelen drew the red sword an inch from its sheath. The steel whispered, a sound like a sighed warning. The sun slipped another finger’s width below the horizon, and the long shadow of the gatehouse stretched toward him, dark and solid as a drawn bowstring. It was a shadow that did not move with the sun, he thought, a shadow that waited.

## Blood on the Southern Wind

The southern wind came first, a warm, salt-tinged breath that carried the scent of jasmine from the hills and the deeper, older smell of damp earth. It was a liar, Kaelen thought. It whispered of home while the ground beneath his boots whispered of death. He stood on the rise, the crimson steel of his sword—*Scarlet’s Kiss*—cool and familiar against his palm. The blade was clean now, but his knuckles were still stained a dark, rusted brown.

Down in the valley, the battle was over. The Northern mercenaries had broken, fleeing into the thinning mist like shadows before dawn. But the silence they left behind was worse than any clamor. It was a silence filled with the sighs of the wounded and the soft, final words of men he’d eaten with just yesterday.

A cough, wet and fragile, drew his eye. Ser Rylan, his oldest shield-brother, was propped against a shattered siege standard, a hand pressed to his side. Kaelen’s steps were heavy, each one sinking slightly into the churned mud.

“Thought you’d ride on ahead,” Rylan rasped, a ghost of his usual grin touching his lips. His eyes were already losing focus, fixed on the vast, indifferent sky.

“The wind changed,” Kaelen said, kneeling. His voice was rough. He didn’t look at the wound; he busied himself with adjusting the man’s cloak, a futile gesture against the coming chill. “Told me to wait.”

Rylan’s chuckle turned into a spasm of pain. “Liar. It told you the roses were blooming at home.” He reached up, his fingers brushing the worn leather of Kaelen’s vambrace. “Go on, then. Take the wind at its word. The South… needs its knight.”

Kaelen closed his eyes. The jasmine scent was stronger now, impossibly sweet, weaving through the copper tang of blood. He saw his sister’s face, her hair the color of ripe wheat. He saw the quiet fields he’d sworn to guard.

He stood, the movement deliberate. He sheathed his sword, the scrape of metal loud in the quiet. He did not look back at the valley of the fallen. Instead, he turned into the southern wind, letting it fill his lungs, wash the grime from his face. It was a promise, and a curse. He was the red sword from the south, and the wind would carry his story—of loss, of duty, of a bravery that tasted of ash and salt—all the way home.

## Dawning of the Unbroken Sun

The dawn did not break so much as it bled, a slow seep of molten copper across the cracked throat of the southern wastes. Sir Kaelen of the Crimson Guard felt the heat before he saw it, a dry hand pressing against his face. His armor, once a brilliant scarlet, was now a tapestry of dust and rust, the leather creaking a lonely song with each shallow breath. He sat on a fallen pillar of forgotten stone, the *Sun-Sunder* across his knees, its blade drinking the early light and holding it, blood-warm and unyielding.

His hand, wrapped in stained linen, traced the fuller of the sword. Not a memory of home—the green hills of his youth were a ghost—but the memory of the oath. *For the sun that never sets.* A promise made in cooler climes, now tested by this endless, bleaching glare.

“It will not cool today,” said Torvin, his squire, voice raspy from the same air that scoured the land. He offered a skin of water, its contents barely a cupful. “The wind has changed. It carries the smell of the salt flats, and something else. Dry stone.”

Kaelen took the skin, his eyes not on the water but on the horizon where the light was hardening the world into sharp, definitive edges. He drank, the water a fleeting rebellion against the thirst that would return. “Then we move before the sun finds its full voice.”

He stood, the movement a testament to will over weariness. The *Sun-Sunder* rose with him, a single, unwavering point of red in the graying land. He did not look back at the ghost of their camp, only forward, toward the jagged silhouette of the Dragon’s Tooth mountains. That was where the path turned, where the stories said the Unbroken Sun still touched the earth each morning, a blessing for those who endured.

Torvin fell into step beside him, the crunch of their boots on sunbaked clay the only sound. “You think it’s just a story?” the boy asked, his youth a fragile thing in this vastness.

Kaelen’s gaze remained fixed ahead, his jaw set. The story was all they had. The story, and the unbroken promise in his hand. The first true beam of dawn struck the peak of the distant mountain, and for a single, breathless moment, the entire world seemed to hold its light. He tightened his grip on the sword hilt, the leather familiar and solid as a heartbeat.

“We will find out,” he said, and his voice, though quiet, cut through the morning’s silence like the first, decisive stroke of a blade. They walked into the light.
